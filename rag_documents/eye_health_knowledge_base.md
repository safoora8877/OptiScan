# Eye Health Knowledge Base for OptiScan AI Assistant

## Model Behavior Guidelines

### Primary Role

The OptiScan AI Assistant is designed to provide general information and educational support regarding eye health, common eye conditions, and related routine issues. It serves as a supplementary tool for patients and healthcare professionals to facilitate informed discussions about eye care.

### Response Principles

1. **Educational Focus**: Provide accurate, evidence-based information drawn from established medical knowledge and guidelines.
2. **Non-Diagnostic**: Never provide specific diagnoses, treatment plans, or medical advice. Always emphasize that responses are for informational purposes only.
3. **Professional Consultation**: Consistently recommend consulting qualified eye care professionals (ophthalmologists, optometrists) for personalized medical advice.
4. **Evidence-Based**: Base all information on current medical literature and guidelines from reputable organizations such as the American Academy of Ophthalmology (AAO), World Health Organization (WHO), and National Institutes of Health (NIH).
5. **Clarity and Accessibility**: Use clear, non-technical language while maintaining medical accuracy. Avoid jargon or explain it when necessary.
6. **Comprehensive Coverage**: Address both common and serious eye conditions, preventive measures, and when to seek professional care.
7. **Cultural Sensitivity**: Provide information that is inclusive and considers diverse populations and healthcare access.
8. **Continuous Learning**: Acknowledge limitations and direct users to current, peer-reviewed sources.

### Interaction Guidelines

- **Question Analysis**: Carefully analyze user queries to identify key concerns and provide relevant, targeted information.
- **Follow-up Questions**: Encourage users to provide more details about symptoms or concerns to offer more relevant general information.
- **Resource Provision**: Suggest reliable resources for further reading and professional consultation.
- **Emergency Situations**: Clearly identify signs of medical emergencies and urge immediate professional medical attention.
- **Privacy and Ethics**: Maintain user privacy and adhere to ethical guidelines for health information provision.

### Limitations and Disclaimers

- This AI assistant does not replace professional medical care or diagnosis.
- Information provided is general and may not apply to individual circumstances.
- Users should always consult healthcare professionals for personal health concerns.
- The assistant cannot access or review personal medical records.

## Common Eye Conditions and Diseases

### Cataracts

**Definition**: A cataract is a clouding of the lens in the eye that affects vision. Most cataracts are related to aging, but they can also be caused by trauma, radiation exposure, or certain medications.

**Symptoms**:

- Blurred or dim vision
- Increased difficulty with vision at night
- Sensitivity to light and glare
- Seeing "halos" around lights
- Frequent changes in eyeglass or contact lens prescription
- Fading or yellowing of colors
- Double vision in a single eye

**Risk Factors**:

- Age (most common after 60)
- Diabetes
- Smoking
- Prolonged exposure to sunlight
- Family history
- Previous eye injury or inflammation
- Certain medications (corticosteroids)

**Impact on Eye Health**: Cataracts can significantly impair vision, leading to reduced quality of life, increased risk of falls, and difficulty performing daily activities.

**Prevention**: Regular eye exams, UV-protective sunglasses, smoking cessation, and managing underlying conditions like diabetes.

**When to See a Doctor**: If experiencing any vision changes, especially sudden onset or rapid progression.

### Glaucoma

**Definition**: Glaucoma is a group of eye conditions that damage the optic nerve, often due to abnormally high pressure in the eye. It is one of the leading causes of blindness worldwide.

**Types**:

- Open-angle glaucoma (most common)
- Angle-closure glaucoma (acute emergency)
- Normal-tension glaucoma
- Secondary glaucoma

**Symptoms**:

- Open-angle: Often asymptomatic in early stages; gradual peripheral vision loss
- Angle-closure: Severe eye pain, headache, blurred vision, halos around lights, nausea
- Both types may cause tunnel vision in advanced stages

**Risk Factors**:

- Age over 60
- Family history
- African, Hispanic, or Asian ancestry
- High eye pressure
- Thin corneas
- Certain medical conditions (diabetes, heart disease)
- Eye injuries

**Impact on Eye Health**: Progressive and irreversible vision loss if untreated. Can lead to complete blindness.

**Prevention**: Regular eye pressure checks, comprehensive eye exams, especially after age 40.

**When to See a Doctor**: Immediate medical attention for acute symptoms; regular screening for those at risk.

### Age-Related Macular Degeneration (AMD)

**Definition**: AMD is a progressive eye disease that affects the macula, the central part of the retina responsible for sharp, central vision.

**Types**:

- Dry AMD (atrophic): More common, slower progression
- Wet AMD (neovascular): Less common but more severe

**Symptoms**:

- Blurred or fuzzy vision
- Straight lines appearing wavy or crooked
- Dark or empty areas in central vision
- Difficulty recognizing faces
- Decreased intensity or brightness of colors

**Risk Factors**:

- Age over 50
- Family history
- Smoking
- Obesity
- Cardiovascular disease
- Prolonged sun exposure
- Light-colored eyes

**Impact on Eye Health**: Central vision loss affects reading, driving, and recognizing faces, significantly impacting independence.

**Prevention**: Healthy diet rich in antioxidants, regular exercise, smoking cessation, UV protection.

**When to See a Doctor**: Any changes in central vision require prompt evaluation.

### Diabetic Retinopathy

**Definition**: Diabetic retinopathy is a complication of diabetes that affects the blood vessels in the retina, potentially leading to vision loss.

**Stages**:

- Mild nonproliferative
- Moderate nonproliferative
- Severe nonproliferative
- Proliferative

**Symptoms**:

- Often asymptomatic in early stages
- Floaters
- Blurred vision
- Dark or empty areas in vision
- Vision loss

**Risk Factors**:

- Duration of diabetes
- Poor blood sugar control
- High blood pressure
- High cholesterol
- Pregnancy
- Tobacco use

**Impact on Eye Health**: Can cause severe vision impairment or blindness if untreated.

**Prevention**: Strict blood sugar control, regular eye exams (annually for diabetics), blood pressure management.

**When to See a Doctor**: All diabetics should have regular eye exams; immediate attention for vision changes.

## Routine Eye Issues and Conditions

### Dry Eyes

**Definition**: Dry eye syndrome occurs when the eyes don't produce enough tears or the tears evaporate too quickly.

**Symptoms**:

- Stinging or burning sensation
- Redness
- Sensitivity to light
- Blurred vision
- Eye fatigue
- Stringy mucus in or around eyes

**Causes**:

- Aging
- Environmental factors (dry air, wind, smoke)
- Screen time
- Certain medications
- Medical conditions (arthritis, Sjögren's syndrome)
- Contact lens wear

**Impact on Eye Health**: Chronic discomfort, potential corneal damage if severe.

**Management**: Artificial tears, warm compresses, lid hygiene, environmental modifications.

### Eye Allergies

**Definition**: Allergic conjunctivitis is an inflammatory response of the conjunctiva to allergens.

**Symptoms**:

- Itchy eyes
- Redness
- Watery discharge
- Swollen eyelids
- Sensitivity to light

**Common Allergens**:

- Pollen
- Dust mites
- Pet dander
- Mold
- Smoke
- Certain cosmetics

**Impact on Eye Health**: Generally temporary, but chronic allergies can lead to complications.

**Management**: Avoid allergens, antihistamine eye drops, cold compresses.

### Conjunctivitis (Pink Eye)

**Definition**: Inflammation of the conjunctiva, commonly caused by infection, allergies, or irritants.

**Types**:

- Viral (highly contagious)
- Bacterial (contagious)
- Allergic
- Chemical/irritant

**Symptoms**:

- Redness
- Discharge (watery for viral, thick/pus-like for bacterial)
- Itching or burning
- Swollen eyelids
- Crusting of eyelids

**Impact on Eye Health**: Usually resolves without long-term damage, but can spread or cause complications.

**Management**: Depends on cause; viral often self-limiting, bacterial may need antibiotics.

### Blepharitis

**Definition**: Inflammation of the eyelids, often involving the eyelash follicles.

**Symptoms**:

- Red, swollen eyelids
- Itching or burning
- Crusting of eyelashes
- Flaking of skin around eyes
- Foreign body sensation

**Causes**:

- Bacterial infection
- Seborrheic dermatitis
- Rosacea
- Poor eyelid hygiene

**Impact on Eye Health**: Chronic condition that can affect tear quality and corneal health.

**Management**: Lid hygiene, warm compresses, antibiotic ointments if needed.

### Floaters and Flashes

**Definition**: Floaters are small specks or clouds that move in your field of vision. Flashes are brief sparks or lightning streaks.

**Causes**:

- Normal aging changes in vitreous
- Retinal tears or detachment
- Posterior vitreous detachment
- Inflammation
- Bleeding in the eye

**Symptoms**:

- Small dots, lines, or cobweb-like shapes
- Brief flashes of light
- Sudden increase in floaters

**Impact on Eye Health**: Usually benign, but can indicate serious conditions like retinal detachment.

**When to See a Doctor**: Sudden onset of many floaters or flashes requires urgent evaluation.

## Eye Health Maintenance and Prevention

### Regular Eye Exams

**Recommended Schedule**:

- Children: At 6 months, 3 years, before school, then annually
- Adults 20-39: Every 5-10 years if no risk factors
- Adults 40-54: Every 2-4 years
- Adults 55-64: Every 1-3 years
- Adults 65+: Every 1-2 years
- Diabetics: Annually
- High-risk individuals: As recommended by eye care professional

### Protective Measures

- UV-blocking sunglasses
- Safety eyewear for hazardous activities
- Proper lighting for reading and work
- Regular breaks during screen time (20-20-20 rule)
- Healthy diet rich in antioxidants (leafy greens, fish, nuts)
- Smoking cessation
- Blood pressure and cholesterol management

### Nutrition for Eye Health

**Key Nutrients**:

- Vitamin A: For retinal health
- Vitamin C: Antioxidant protection
- Vitamin E: Protects cell membranes
- Zinc: Supports retinal function
- Lutein and Zeaxanthin: Protect macula
- Omega-3 fatty acids: Reduce dry eye risk

**Food Sources**:

- Leafy greens (spinach, kale)
- Fatty fish (salmon, tuna)
- Eggs
- Nuts and seeds
- Citrus fruits
- Carrots and sweet potatoes

### Screen Time and Digital Eye Strain

**Symptoms**:

- Eye fatigue
- Headaches
- Blurred vision
- Dry eyes
- Neck and shoulder pain

**Prevention**:

- 20-20-20 rule: Every 20 minutes, look 20 feet away for 20 seconds
- Proper screen positioning
- Adequate lighting
- Anti-glare screens
- Regular blinking exercises

## When to Seek Emergency Care

### Immediate Medical Attention Required for:

- Sudden vision loss
- Severe eye pain
- Flashing lights or sudden increase in floaters
- Double vision
- Trauma to the eye
- Chemical exposure
- Foreign body in eye
- Signs of infection with fever

### Urgent Care (Within 24-48 hours):

- Moderate eye pain
- Significant vision changes
- Persistent redness or discharge
- Swelling around eyes
- New onset of halos around lights

## Pediatric Eye Health

### Common Childhood Eye Issues

- Amblyopia (lazy eye)
- Strabismus (crossed eyes)
- Refractive errors
- Congenital cataracts
- Retinopathy of prematurity

### Importance of Early Detection

- Vision develops rapidly in first few years
- Early intervention prevents permanent vision loss
- Many conditions are treatable if caught early

### Screening Recommendations

- Newborn screening for congenital conditions
- Vision screening at well-child visits
- Comprehensive eye exam by age 1
- Regular screenings through school years

## Occupational and Environmental Eye Health

### Workplace Eye Safety

- Proper protective eyewear for hazardous jobs
- Regular eye exams for workers exposed to risks
- Awareness of occupational eye injuries

### Environmental Factors

- Air pollution and eye irritation
- Extreme weather conditions
- Indoor air quality
- Occupational exposures (chemicals, radiation)

## Aging and Eye Health

### Age-Related Changes

- Presbyopia (difficulty focusing on near objects)
- Reduced tear production
- Yellowing of lens
- Decreased pupil size
- Changes in color perception

### Special Considerations for Seniors

- Increased risk of chronic conditions
- Medication side effects on vision
- Mobility and fall prevention
- Low vision rehabilitation
